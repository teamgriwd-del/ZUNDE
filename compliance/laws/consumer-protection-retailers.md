# Consumer Protection Act [Chapter 14:44] — For Retailers

> Not legal advice. See the [disclaimer](../README.md).

## What it is

The Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (Act No. 5 of 2019, current citation Chapter 14:44) is Zimbabwe's general consumer-rights statute. It applies broadly to suppliers of goods and services — including retailers of livestock and agricultural produce operating through PFUMA — and sets out obligations around disclosure, pricing, product quality, and fair dealing, alongside an enforcement/penalty regime.

## Key obligations relevant to a livestock/agri-produce Retailer

### 1. Disclosure of information (Section 26, per sources reviewed)
Every supplier of goods or services must disclose **complete and truthful information** about what they're selling, in **plain and understandable language**, so a consumer can genuinely understand the terms of the transaction. This applies to both formal and informal businesses — meaning a small-scale Retailer on PFUMA is just as bound as a large agro-dealer. For livestock/agri-produce specifically, this plausibly extends to disclosing things like the animal's health status, origin, and any known issues — the platform should not assume disclosure duties stop at price and description alone.

### 2. Price disclosure
- Retailers must **display a price** on goods offered for sale — failing to do so is a criminal offence under the Act.
- The **price displayed must be the price actually charged** at the point of sale.
- If multiple prices are shown concurrently for the same item, the **consumer is entitled to pay the lowest price displayed**.

### 3. Disclosure of reconditioned/grey-market goods, and disclosure by intermediaries
The Act includes specific provisions requiring disclosure when goods are reconditioned or grey-market, and imposes disclosure duties on intermediaries (relevant if PFUMA itself, or a Supplier/Retailer acting as a middleman, is treated as an intermediary in a transaction).

### 4. Quality guarantees and returns
Suppliers implicitly warrant that goods sold are **safe, free from defects, and of fair value**. Consumers have a right to return defective goods **within six months of delivery**, without penalty and at the supplier's risk/expense, and the supplier must repair, replace, or refund for substandard goods.

### 5. Prohibition on unfair practices
Retailers may not use **physical force, coercion, undue influence, duress, harassment, or unfair tactics** in dealing with consumers. Signage or disclaimers that purport to deny consumer rights — e.g. blanket "no refund" policies — are **explicitly prohibited**. False or misleading marketing claims are also forbidden.

### 6. Penalties
Violations can lead to **fines, imprisonment, and/or damages**, determined by a court or arbitrator, with sources indicating a trend toward stricter enforcement.

## What this means for PFUMA

- **Retailer** listings should require a clear, honest description of what's being sold (species, age/weight where relevant, health status, origin/documentation available) and a **fixed, visible price** — no bait pricing or hidden fee structures.
- The platform should not allow Retailer accounts to post blanket "no refund/no returns" language for defective goods, since the Act renders such disclaimers void as against a consumer's statutory rights.
- Where a Retailer is reselling animals sourced from a Farmer, the disclosure duty likely flows through — the Retailer should be encouraged/required to pass on known health/origin information rather than presenting a resold animal as if its history were unknown.
- The AI assistant should be able to tell a Farmer or buyer, in plain terms, "a registered retailer must tell you X, Y, Z about what you're buying, and cannot legally refuse all returns" — while being clear this describes the general legal framework and not a platform-specific guarantee.
